## What Actually Matters

### Annual or lifetime limits — know yours before you need them

Many policies cap annual reimbursement at $2,500–$5,000 per year. For a dog with cancer or a cat with chronic kidney disease, this cap is reached in months. Policies with unlimited annual benefits (Healthy Paws, Trupanion) or $10,000+ limits are meaningfully different from policies with $2,500 caps — even if the monthly premium difference seems small. The $20/month savings on a capped policy costs you $5,000+ if a serious condition develops.

### Waiting periods — especially for illness

Every pet insurance policy has a waiting period between enrollment and coverage start. Accident coverage typically activates within 24–48 hours. Illness coverage typically has a 14-day waiting period. Some policies have 6–12 month waiting periods specifically for orthopedic conditions. Enroll early — waiting until your pet is sick means the illness is now a pre-existing condition excluded from coverage.

### Pre-existing condition exclusions — the most important fine print

A condition diagnosed or showing symptoms before your policy start date is typically excluded permanently. This includes: "bilateral" conditions (if one hip has been diagnosed with dysplasia, some policies exclude the other hip). Some policies use "look-back periods" — reviewing 12 months of records before enrollment. The critical action: enroll young, before any conditions develop. An 8-week-old puppy enrolled at first vet visit has the cleanest possible record.

### Reimbursement model — actual cost vs. benefit schedule

Two reimbursement models exist: (1) actual cost reimbursement — you pay the bill, submit the claim, receive a percentage (usually 70–90%) of the actual invoice back. (2) benefit schedule reimbursement — the policy pays a fixed amount per procedure regardless of actual cost. In markets where veterinary fees have risen 40%+ in five years, benefit schedule policies routinely reimburse 30–50 cents on the dollar. Actual cost reimbursement is the significantly superior model.

## What to Avoid

- Annual coverage limits under $5,000 — inadequate for any serious illness or emergency
- Benefit schedule reimbursement models — pay far less than actual cost in high-cost veterinary markets
- Policies with 6–12 month waiting periods for orthopedic conditions (especially for large breeds)
- "Wellness" add-ons that cost more than the reimbursed wellness care — often a net negative value proposition
- Enrolling after symptoms have appeared — that condition is now pre-existing and excluded

## FAQ

### When should I get pet insurance?

As early as possible — ideally at or before the first veterinary visit. Most policies enroll pets as young as 8 weeks old. The younger the enrollment, the fewer documented conditions that could trigger pre-existing exclusions. The AVMA reports the most common regret among pet insurance purchasers is waiting until the pet was sick to enroll.

### What's typically not covered by pet insurance?

Pre-existing conditions (those diagnosed or with symptoms before policy start), elective procedures (cosmetic surgery, ear cropping, declawing), preventive care without a wellness add-on (vaccines, flea prevention, annual exams), and in many policies: dental disease beyond accidental dental injury. Read the exclusions before purchasing, not after.

### Is pet insurance worth it?

The math depends on your pet's risk profile and your financial situation. For owners who cannot cover a $5,000 emergency without financial stress: yes, unambiguously. For owners with large liquid emergency funds: it's effectively gambling on whether your pet's lifetime vet costs exceed premiums paid. Statistically, approximately 1 in 3 pets will have a major veterinary expense ($2,000+) in their lifetime. Pet insurance is most clearly worth it for: breeds with high health risk profiles, puppies and kittens before conditions develop, and owners without emergency funds.

### How do I file a claim?

Submit the itemized veterinary invoice and medical records to your insurer within the timeframe specified in your policy (typically 30–90 days of the treatment date). Most modern insurers accept email or app submission. Processing time varies from 5 days (Figo) to 30 days (some older providers). Direct payment providers (Trupanion at enrolled practices) handle this automatically at the time of service.

### Can I use any vet with pet insurance?

Yes — virtually all pet insurance policies reimburse for care from any licensed veterinarian in the US and Canada. You don't have a "network" restriction the way human health insurance works. Pay your vet, submit the claim, receive reimbursement. The only exception is direct-payment models (Trupanion), which require the practice to be enrolled in their network for direct billing — though you can still self-pay and get reimbursed at non-enrolled practices.
