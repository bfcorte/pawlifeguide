## Why This Matters

Guinea pigs are prey animals with specific biological requirements that differ substantially from dogs and cats. Their teeth grow continuously — dental disease is the #1 health problem in domestic guinea pigs, directly caused by insufficient hay intake. Their inability to produce Vitamin C (one of very few mammals with this limitation, shared only with humans and primates) means scurvy is a real, common, and entirely preventable cause of death in pet guinea pigs.

Dr. Katherine Quesenberry, DVM at the Animal Medical Center in New York and the leading US authority on exotic small mammals, describes guinea pigs as "one of the most misrepresented pets in the retail environment — the gap between what stores sell and what the animal actually needs is significant."

## Before You Start

Before bringing a guinea pig home:

- **Buy the right cage first**: Most pet store "starter kits" are 3–4 sq ft. The minimum for one guinea pig is 7.5 sq ft of floor space; for two, 10.5 sq ft. C&C cages (grid panels + coroplast) are the most common solution.
- **Get two, not one**: Guinea pigs are highly social and suffer measurable stress when kept alone. The Humane Society recommends same-sex pairs at minimum. Rescues often adopt pairs together.
- **Have a vet identified before you need one**: Exotic animal vets who treat guinea pigs are not the same as standard dog/cat vets. Find one in your area before a health issue arises — emergencies are not the time to search.
- **Understand the lifespan commitment**: 4–7 years. Guinea pigs are not short-term pets.

## Step-by-Step Guide

### Step 1: Set up the habitat correctly — size and structure

The cage determines everything downstream. A guinea pig in a 2 sq ft pet store cage will be sedentary, stressed, and prone to illness. The minimum for one pig is 7.5 sq ft of unobstructed floor space — for two pigs (which is what you should have), 10.5 sq ft.

Line the floor with paper bedding at least 3 inches deep. Cedar and pine shavings are toxic — the aromatic oils cause chronic respiratory disease. Fleece liners are an alternative but require daily spot-cleaning. Provide at minimum two hideouts per pig so neither animal can monopolize a hiding space.

### Step 2: Establish the diet — 80% hay, always available

Timothy hay should be available 24/7 and make up 80% of the diet. This is not optional — the constant chewing action is the only thing that prevents the dental overgrowth that causes guinea pigs to stop eating and slowly starve. A hay rack keeps it clean and off the cage floor.

Pellets: 1/8 cup per pig per day of plain timothy-based pellets (not mixes with seeds and colored pieces — those are junk food that pigs sort out, leaving the nutritious pellets behind). Fresh vegetables daily for Vitamin C: half a bell pepper (highest Vit C density per ounce), dark leafy greens, small amounts of parsley. Avoid iceberg lettuce (no nutrition), spinach in large amounts (oxalates), and fruit except as rare treats.

### Step 3: Provide Vitamin C correctly

This is where most owners fail. Guinea pigs need 10–30mg of Vitamin C per day. Drops in water bottles degrade within hours and provide inconsistent dosing. The correct approach: fresh vegetables with high Vitamin C content daily (half a bell pepper provides approximately 95mg), or a small piece of high-quality pellet that has stabilized Vitamin C.

Signs of scurvy develop within 2–3 weeks of Vitamin C deficiency: reluctance to move, swollen joints, rough coat, weight loss. It's reversible if caught early and fatal if not.

### Step 4: Handle correctly from the first week

Guinea pigs are prey animals — they are hardwired to fear anything approaching from above. Always approach from the side, scoop from below with both hands supporting the hindquarters, and hold against your body. Never grip by the scruff or restrain a struggling animal.

Daily 15–20 minute handling sessions during the first month build trust. Signs a guinea pig is comfortable: relaxed body, "purring" (a low vibration), exploring without freezing. Signs of stress: chattering teeth (back off immediately), freezing completely, rapid breathing.

### Step 5: Learn their vocalizations — they communicate clearly

- **Wheeking** (high-pitched squeal): anticipation, usually food-related
- **Purring** (low rumble while being held): contentment
- **Chuttering** (rapid teeth chattering): warning — back off
- **Rumblestrutting** (low rumble while strutting): dominance behavior between pigs
- **Whining**: discomfort or protest

Understanding these signals tells you when the animal is calm enough to handle, when two pigs are establishing hierarchy (normal), and when something is wrong.

## Mistakes That Set You Back

- **Cedar or pine shavings as bedding** — aromatic oils cause chronic respiratory disease; use paper bedding only
- **Keeping a single guinea pig** — documented stress response measurably affects health and lifespan
- **Vitamin C drops in water** — degrades too fast; use fresh vegetables or fresh pellets
- **Feeding mixes with seeds and colored bits** — guinea pigs eat the junk and leave the nutrition
- **Handling by approaching from above** — triggers prey panic; always approach from the side
- **Treating dental problems at home** — dental disease requires an exotic vet; overgrown molars will cause death if untreated

## Expert Perspective

The Association of Exotic Mammal Veterinarians (AEMV) recommends annual wellness exams for guinea pigs from a vet with exotic small mammal experience — not a general practice dog/cat vet. Dental exams under anesthesia are often necessary to assess molar health, which cannot be evaluated by visual inspection alone. Finding this vet *before* an emergency is one of the most important things you can do for a guinea pig's long-term health.

## FAQ

### How big does a guinea pig cage really need to be?

At minimum, 7.5 sq ft of unobstructed floor space for one pig — and you should have two. Most pet store cages are 3–4 sq ft, which is inadequate. C&C cages (coroplast base, grid panels) are the standard solution: a 2×4 grid layout gives approximately 8 sq ft and costs $50–$80 to build.

### Can guinea pigs live alone?

Technically yes, but it's not recommended. Guinea pigs are highly social — lone guinea pigs display measurable stress behaviors and have shorter average lifespans than paired guinea pigs in studies. Many shelters and rescues require pair adoptions. If you already have one pig, most rescues will help you find a compatible companion.

### What vegetables are safe for guinea pigs?

Bell peppers (all colors, highest Vitamin C content), romaine lettuce, green leaf lettuce, cilantro, parsley (in moderation — high calcium), cucumber, zucchini. Avoid: iceberg lettuce (no nutrition), potatoes, onions, garlic, rhubarb. Fruit is high in sugar — limit to a small piece 2–3 times per week maximum.

### How do I know if my guinea pig is sick?

Guinea pigs hide illness as a survival mechanism — by the time symptoms are visible, the condition is often advanced. Warning signs: weight loss (weigh weekly on a kitchen scale — more than 2oz loss in a week warrants a vet visit), changes in droppings, reduced hay intake, labored breathing, discharge from eyes or nose, reluctance to move. A healthy guinea pig is vocal, active, and eating hay continuously.

### Do guinea pigs need baths?

Rarely. Guinea pigs groom themselves and each other. A bath is appropriate only if the pig has gotten into something sticky or has a skin condition requiring medicated shampoo. Use lukewarm water, guinea pig-safe shampoo, and dry completely before returning to the cage — they chill easily.
