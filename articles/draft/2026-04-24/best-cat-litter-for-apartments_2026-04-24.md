## What Actually Matters

### Dust level — non-negotiable in small spaces

Clay litter produces fine silica dust during pouring and scooping. In a house, this disperses. In a 600 sq ft apartment, you and your cat breathe it for hours. Look for litters verified at "99% dust-free" — not just labeled. Low-dust formulas use a different granule size and binding process.

### Clumping strength — harder clumps mean less odor

Weak clumps crumble during scooping, releasing trapped ammonia into the air. Hard clumps that hold their shape contain odor until disposal. This is the most underrated factor in litter odor control — a hard-clumping unscented litter outperforms a soft-clumping scented one in apartments every time.

### Scent strategy — unscented beats scented in enclosed spaces

Heavy artificial fragrance masks odor on retail shelves. In daily use in a small apartment, scented litters create a layered problem: ammonia underneath, artificial perfume on top. Unscented litters that neutralize odor biochemically (activated charcoal, plant-based neutralizers) outperform scented options consistently in limited-ventilation environments.

### Weight per coverage area

A 40-lb bag of clay covers the same usage as a 14-lb bag of concentrated corn litter. If you live on the 4th floor without an elevator, that weight differential is a real quality-of-life factor.

## What to Avoid

- Scented litters with artificial fragrance — creates layered odor problem in enclosed spaces
- High-dust clay not labeled "dust-free" — respiratory irritant in small apartments
- Cheap clumping litters that crumble — releases ammonia on scoop, defeats the purpose
- Crystal silica in multi-cat homes — high per-use cost and poor clumping

## FAQ

### How often do I need to fully replace litter in an apartment?

With hard-clumping litter and daily scooping, a full change every 3–4 weeks is typical for one cat. The signal: litter smells between scoops despite being clean. In apartments with limited ventilation, err toward the shorter end.

### Are scented litters bad for cats?

Not inherently — but cats reject them at higher rates. Their sense of smell is 14x more sensitive than ours. A fragrance that smells mild to you is potentially overwhelming to the cat. Litter box avoidance is the most common behavioral consequence. If your cat is avoiding the box, switching to unscented is the first troubleshooting step.

### Is clumping or non-clumping better for apartments?

Clumping, consistently. Non-clumping litters absorb urine into granules without forming discrete removable clumps — complete odor containment between full changes is impossible. Hard-clumping litters let you remove exactly the affected material. In a small space where odor concentrates fast, the daily removal of contained clumps makes a significant difference.

### What's the right litter box size for an apartment?

1.5x the length of your cat from nose to tail. Most commercial boxes are too small — the cat needs space to turn around, dig, and cover without hanging over the edge. Larger boxes also reduce litter tracking onto apartment floors. Uncovered boxes are preferred by most cats; covered boxes concentrate ammonia at nose level for the cat using them.

### Can I use pellet litter to reduce dust?

Yes. Pine pellets are extremely low dust and control ammonia well. Trade-off: they don't clump, requiring more frequent full changes, and some cats refuse the texture. If dust is your primary concern and your cat accepts it, pine pellets are a valid apartment option.
