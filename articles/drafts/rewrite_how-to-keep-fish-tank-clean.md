# The Right Way to Keep a Fish Tank Clean (2026 Guide)

A properly maintained aquarium should never smell. If yours does, it means the biological, mechanical, or chemical filtration has broken down — or never fully established. New fishkeepers often treat tank maintenance as cleaning: scrubbing algae, replacing water when it looks murky, changing filter cartridges on a schedule. Experienced fishkeepers treat it as ecosystem management: the goal is not a visually clean tank but a biologically stable one, and those are different things.

The most destructive thing you can do to an aquarium is a complete water change and simultaneous filter cleaning. Both actions together collapse the nitrogen cycle and restart the tank from zero — causing an ammonia spike that kills fish within 24–48 hours. Understanding why prevents the most common maintenance mistakes.

## Why This Matters

Fish produce ammonia continuously through respiration and waste. The beneficial bacteria that convert ammonia to nitrate — established during the nitrogen cycle — live in the filter media and substrate, not in the water. These bacteria are the reason the tank is livable. Their health determines whether your tank is stable or crash-prone. Every maintenance mistake that kills fish traces back to one of three errors: disrupting the bacterial colony, failing to remove accumulated waste before it degrades, or changing too much water chemistry too fast.

A stable, clean tank requires about 30 minutes per week and 1–2 hours per month. It is not complicated — but the specific sequence matters.

## Before You Start

- **Know your nitrogen cycle status**: If the tank was cycled properly, you have established bacterial colonies. If it was not cycled, or was set up in the last 4 weeks without cycling, address that first — maintenance routines don't work on an uncycled tank.
- **Have the right tools**: gravel vacuum (siphon), algae scraper, bucket designated for tank use only (no soap residue), and dechlorinator for every water change.
- **Know your tap water**: chlorine kills bacterial colonies; chloramine requires a dechlorinator that specifically addresses it (not just chlorine). Check with your local water utility or use a comprehensive dechlorinator like Seachem Prime that handles both.

## Step-by-Step Guide

### Step 1: Weekly water changes — 25–30% of total volume

Change 25–30% of the tank water every week using dechlorinated water matched to the tank's temperature (within 2°F). The purpose: dilute accumulated nitrate, replenish trace minerals, and remove dissolved organic compounds before they decompose.

Do not change more than 50% at once in an established tank — large water changes stress fish through rapid pH and chemistry shifts and can disrupt the bacterial colony if the new water's chemistry differs significantly from the tank water.

### Step 2: Gravel vacuuming — remove waste from the substrate

During the weekly water change, use a gravel vacuum (siphon) to pull waste from the substrate. The vacuum draws water and debris out simultaneously — the water change and gravel cleaning happen in the same operation. You do not need to vacuum the entire gravel every week; rotate sections (front half one week, back half the next). Complete gravel vacuuming monthly.

Deeply planted tanks: vacuum more gently and less thoroughly — disturbing plant roots damages the plants. Bare-bottom tanks: the entire floor can be vacuumed fully each week.

### Step 3: Filter maintenance — never clean it with tap water

Every 4–6 weeks, remove filter media and rinse it in a bucket of used tank water (taken from the tank during a water change). The purpose is to remove clogging debris while preserving the bacterial colony. Never use tap water — the chlorine kills the beneficial bacteria. Never wash filter media with soap.

If using cartridge-style filters: replace only one cartridge at a time when it's fully degraded, not on a schedule. Many manufacturers recommend monthly cartridge replacement — this is convenient for selling cartridges, not correct for biological filtration. A functioning filter cartridge should be left in place as long as water flows through it adequately.

### Step 4: Algae management — remove, don't eliminate

Algae is not inherently a problem — it's a natural part of a healthy aquarium ecosystem. Light green algae on glass is removed cosmetically with an algae scraper before it builds up. Black beard algae (dark, brush-like growth on decor and plants) indicates elevated phosphate or inconsistent lighting — treat the cause, not just the symptom.

Do not use algae-killing chemicals in an established planted or fish-inhabited tank. They typically harm plants and beneficial bacteria alongside the algae.

### Step 5: Monthly water testing — track, don't guess

Test ammonia, nitrite, and nitrate monthly with a liquid test kit. Healthy established tank readings: ammonia 0, nitrite 0, nitrate under 40 ppm. If ammonia or nitrite are above 0, the bacterial colony is compromised — do a 50% water change immediately and investigate the cause (overfeeding, dead fish, filter failure). Nitrate above 40 ppm indicates insufficient water change frequency — increase to twice weekly.

## Mistakes That Set You Back

- **Complete water change + complete filter cleaning on the same day** — destroys the bacterial colony, causes ammonia spike
- **Cleaning filter media with tap water** — chlorine kills beneficial bacteria; use only tank water
- **Replacing entire filter cartridges monthly** — removes most of the bacterial colony with each replacement
- **Overfeeding** — uneaten food decomposes, producing ammonia spikes; feed only what the fish consume in 2–3 minutes
- **Temperature mismatch during water changes** — cold water shocking warm-water fish causes immune suppression

## FAQ

### How often should I change the water in my fish tank?

25–30% weekly is the standard for stocked tanks. Lightly stocked tanks (fewer fish than the recommended bioload) can get away with 25% every two weeks. Heavily stocked tanks may need twice-weekly changes. The signal: if nitrate rises above 40 ppm between changes, increase frequency. If fish are visually healthy and parameters are stable, your current schedule is working.

### Why does my tank smell bad after a water change?

Usually one of three causes: the new water was added too cold (shocking the system), the gravel was disturbed releasing trapped waste without full removal, or the new water chemistry differed significantly from the tank water. Test ammonia immediately after a malodorous water change — if it's elevated, do another partial change with properly dechlorinated, temperature-matched water.

### Can I use tap water directly from the faucet?

No — tap water contains chlorine (or chloramine in many municipalities), which kills beneficial bacteria and stresses fish directly. Always add dechlorinator to the new water before or simultaneously with adding it to the tank. Seachem Prime is the most widely recommended: it neutralizes chlorine, chloramine, and heavy metals, and detoxifies low levels of ammonia for 24 hours.

### How do I clean artificial plants and decorations?

Remove them during a water change. Rinse in used tank water (not tap water) and scrub with a toothbrush designated for tank use. For stubborn algae, a 10% bleach solution for 15 minutes followed by thorough rinsing and a 24-hour air dry will kill everything on the surface. Never return bleach-treated items to the tank without verifying no bleach residue remains — use a sodium thiosulfate dip to neutralize.

### My water is always cloudy — what's causing it?

Three common causes: bacterial bloom (white/gray cloudiness in a newly set up tank — normal, clears in 1–2 weeks), green water algae bloom (green cloudiness — caused by excess light or nutrients, treat by reducing light to 8 hours and increasing water change frequency), or suspended particles (tan/brown cloudiness — usually from overfeeding or disturbed substrate, address with more frequent gravel vacuuming).
