## What Actually Matters

### Moisture — the most important factor for indoor cats

Cats evolved as desert hunters whose hydration came almost entirely from prey. A mouse is approximately 70% water. Dry cat food is 8–10% water. Indoor cats on a dry-only diet are in a state of chronic mild dehydration that strains kidneys over years. Wet food at 75–80% moisture is the closest thing to biologically appropriate hydration available in commercial cat food.

### Named animal protein in the first ingredient

The ingredient list is ordered by weight before processing. "Chicken" as the first ingredient means chicken is the primary component. "Meat by-products" or "poultry digest" without a named animal species is a quality signal in the wrong direction. Cats are obligate carnivores — their livers cannot convert plant amino acids into the nutrients they require, so the protein source being a named animal matters, not just the crude protein percentage.

### Taurine listed explicitly

Cats cannot synthesize taurine — it must come from their food. Deficiency causes dilated cardiomyopathy (an irreversible heart condition) and progressive retinal degeneration leading to blindness. Every reputable wet cat food includes it, but checking the ingredient panel takes five seconds and is worth doing.

### No carrageenan

Carrageenan is a seaweed-derived thickener used in many wet cat foods to achieve the pâté texture. Multiple animal studies have linked it to intestinal inflammation and gastrointestinal lesions. It's not banned by AAFCO, but veterinary nutritionists increasingly recommend avoiding it. It appears in the ingredient list — check before buying.

## What to Avoid

- Dry food as the sole diet — chronic dehydration over years
- First ingredient is a grain, broth, or unnamed "meat by-products"
- Carrageenan in the ingredients
- No AAFCO "complete and balanced" statement on the label
- Heavy artificial colors — cats cannot perceive most colors, so dyes serve the buyer's eye, not the cat's health

## Expert Perspective

The World Small Animal Veterinary Association (WSAVA) recommends choosing cat food from companies that employ full-time board-certified veterinary nutritionists and conduct AAFCO feeding trials — not just nutritional analysis. A feeding trial means cats actually ate the food for 26 weeks under veterinary supervision and maintained health. Nutritional analysis means the food meets calculated targets on paper. The distinction is real: Purina, Hill's, and Royal Canin conduct feeding trials. Many boutique brands do not.

## FAQ

### How much wet food should I feed my indoor cat daily?

Most adult indoor cats need 4–6 oz of wet food per day, split into two meals. A 10-lb adult cat at healthy weight needs approximately 180–200 kcal/day — check the caloric density on the can and calculate from there. Bag guidelines typically run generous; the rib test (you should feel each rib without pressing, but not see them) is a more reliable weight indicator than the feeding chart.

### Can I mix wet and dry food for my indoor cat?

Yes — many vets recommend a hybrid approach. Wet food as the primary diet for hydration; a small amount of dry food for dental texture. If mixing, calculate total daily calories from both to avoid overfeeding. A common starting ratio: 75% of calories from wet, 25% from dry. Adjust based on weight and your vet's guidance.

### Is grain-free wet cat food better for indoor cats?

Not necessarily. Cats do not require grain-free food, and the grain-free marketing trend was driven by consumer perception rather than veterinary science. What matters: named animal protein first, adequate moisture, no carrageenan. A grain-containing food that meets those criteria outperforms a grain-free food that doesn't.

### My cat refuses wet food — how do I transition?

Slowly. Start by mixing 10% wet into the dry food. Hold that ratio for 3–4 days, then increase to 25%, 50%, 75%, 100% over 2–3 weeks. Warming wet food slightly (10 seconds in microwave, stir well) increases palatability for resistant cats. The broth toppers work as a first step for cats that refuse even mixed food.

### Does wet food cause dental problems in cats?

This is a persistent myth. Studies do not support the claim that wet food significantly increases dental disease compared to dry food. Dental health in cats depends primarily on genetics and regular veterinary dental care — not food texture. Dry kibble is too soft to meaningfully scrub teeth. If dental health is a concern, enzymatic dental treats or veterinary brushing are more effective strategies.
