## What Actually Matters

### Named animal protein is the first ingredient

The ingredient list is ordered by weight before processing. "Chicken" means chicken is the primary component. "Poultry by-products" or "meat digest" without a named species is a cost-reduction signal. Cats are obligate carnivores — their metabolic pathways require amino acids found only in animal tissue. The specific animal matters because different proteins have different amino acid profiles.

### Moisture content of 75% or higher

Pâté varieties typically hit 78–82% moisture. Chunks-in-gravy varieties often run 65–75% because the gravy adds volume without proportional water content. For cats being switched from dry food for kidney or urinary health reasons, the 78%+ range of pâtés is the most effective intervention.

### Taurine explicitly listed

Cats cannot synthesize taurine. Deficiency causes dilated cardiomyopathy — an irreversible, fatal heart condition — and retinal degeneration leading to permanent blindness. Every AAFCO-compliant wet cat food includes it, but it should appear in the ingredient list. If a food doesn't list taurine and doesn't carry an AAFCO complete and balanced statement, don't buy it.

### No carrageenan

Carrageenan is a red algae extract used as a thickener in many pâté-style wet foods. Animal studies have consistently linked it to intestinal inflammation and gastrointestinal lesions. It is not banned by AAFCO, but the Cornucopia Institute, multiple veterinary nutritionists, and Consumer Reports have flagged it as a concern worth avoiding. It appears by name in the ingredients — it takes ten seconds to check.

## What to Avoid

- "Meat by-products" without a named species as the first ingredient
- Carrageenan anywhere in the ingredient list
- Moisture content below 70% in foods marketed as "wet"
- No AAFCO complete and balanced statement
- Artificial colors — Red 40, Yellow 5 — cats do not benefit from colored food

## Expert Perspective

The World Small Animal Veterinary Association (WSAVA) publishes specific criteria for evaluating pet food companies — not just products. Their key questions: Does the company employ a full-time veterinary nutritionist with board certification? Do they conduct AAFCO feeding trials? Do they have a quality control program with pre- and post-market testing? Among major brands, Purina, Hill's, and Royal Canin answer yes to all three. Many boutique and natural brands do not — and "boutique, exotic ingredient, grain-free" has been flagged by the FDA as a category associated with diet-related heart disease in dogs.

## FAQ

### How much wet food does a cat need daily?

Most adult cats need 4–6 oz of wet food daily (two 3-oz cans or equivalent), split into two meals. For an exact amount: multiply your cat's healthy target weight in pounds by 20 kcal. A 10-lb cat needs roughly 200 kcal/day. Check the caloric density on the specific can — it varies from 80 to 120+ kcal per 3-oz serving.

### Can I feed my cat only wet food?

Yes, and many veterinary nutritionists recommend it as the primary or sole diet for adult cats, particularly for kidney health. Wet food provides adequate water, protein, and nutrients without relying on carbohydrate-dense fillers. The only consideration: dental health requires supplementation through enzymatic dental treats or brushing, since wet food alone provides no mechanical cleaning action.

### Why does my cat eat wet food but leave chunks behind?

This is a texture preference, not a quality signal. Cats that prefer smooth pâté will push chunks aside. Cats that prefer texture will eat chunks but ignore the smooth base. Most manufacturers offer both styles — identify your cat's preference and buy accordingly. Some cats prefer pâté at one stage of life and textured food at another.

### Is expensive wet cat food actually better?

At the premium level, the quality gap is real but narrower than marketing suggests. The meaningful difference between mid-range ($0.30–0.50/oz) and premium ($0.60–0.90/oz) is usually: more specific protein sourcing, stricter quality control, and in some cases published research. Within the premium tier, differences are largely marketing. Focus on the ingredient criteria above rather than price alone.

### How do I store open cans of wet cat food?

Refrigerate open cans covered (or transfer to a sealed container) for up to 5 days. Serve at room temperature or slightly warm — cats evolved eating fresh prey at 100°F and often reject cold food. Warming for 10–15 seconds in the microwave and stirring thoroughly before serving significantly improves acceptance rates, especially during diet transitions.
