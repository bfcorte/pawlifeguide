## The Fiber Floor: Why Most Pellets Fail at the Most Important Thing

Rabbits are hindgut fermenters — their cecum (a fermentation chamber between the small and large intestine) processes fiber through a bacterial colony that is exquisitely sensitive to diet changes. When fiber drops below threshold, that bacterial population destabilizes. Gas builds. Gut motility slows. What follows is GI stasis, which requires emergency veterinary intervention costing $300–$800 and has a mortality rate that veterinary literature places between 20% and 60% depending on how quickly it's caught.

The fiber thresholds that actually matter are not just about the total crude fiber number. You want to distinguish between digestible and indigestible fiber. Indigestible fiber — primarily cellulose and hemicellulose from hay — is what physically keeps the gut moving. Digestible fiber feeds the cecal bacteria. A high-quality rabbit pellet should be made primarily from Timothy hay or another grass hay, which supplies both. Alfalfa-based pellets, by contrast, are high in digestible fiber but low in the indigestible fraction — and they're also dramatically higher in calcium, which creates separate problems we'll cover in the next section.

When reading a pellet label, look for Timothy hay, orchard grass, or oat hay as the first ingredient. The guaranteed analysis should show crude fiber at minimum 18%, with 22–25% being the gold standard for adult rabbits. A crude fiber percentage of 14% or lower — which appears on several major supermarket brands — is functionally inadequate for an adult rabbit's long-term gut health.

The best formulas achieve high fiber without bulking with low-nutrition filler like beet pulp or soy hulls as the primary fiber source. Soy hulls are fermentable rather than indigestible, which means they feed bacteria but don't provide the gut-motility function of true grass fiber. A food listing soy hulls as its second ingredient after corn is not a high-fiber pellet in any meaningful sense — it's a high-fermentable-fiber pellet, which is a very different thing.

One formula that correctly prioritizes grass hay fiber: its guaranteed analysis lands at 25% crude fiber, the protein sits at 14%, and the first ingredient is Timothy grass meal. The pellet profile is consistent with what rabbit-specialist veterinarians recommend for maintenance feeding in adult rabbits aged 1 year and older.

## The Protein and Calcium Problem Nobody Talks About

The fiber conversation dominates rabbit nutrition discussions, but protein and calcium are where long-term organ damage accumulates silently. Most rabbit owners never connect the dots between diet and the kidney disease that shortens their pet's life in its senior years.

Protein: Adult rabbits (over 12 months) need 12–14% crude protein. Young rabbits under 6 months can tolerate up to 16% because they're still building muscle and bone. The problem is that many "complete rabbit foods" are formulated with alfalfa as a primary protein source, pushing protein to 18–22%. Feeding that level of protein to an adult rabbit long-term places chronic oxidative stress on the kidneys — the same mechanism documented in cats and dogs fed excessive dietary protein for years. Rabbit kidneys are not designed to process significant nitrogen waste loads indefinitely.

Calcium: This is where alfalfa-based pellets cause compounded damage. Alfalfa contains between 1.2% and 1.5% calcium by dry weight. Timothy hay contains 0.4–0.6%. Adult rabbits excrete excess calcium through the urinary tract — uniquely, rabbits excrete calcium renally rather than via the intestines like most mammals. This means excess dietary calcium precipitates directly in the bladder and kidneys, forming the "bladder sludge" and urinary calculi that cause significant pain and, in severe cases, require surgical removal. The appropriate calcium range for adult rabbits is 0.5–1.0% of dry matter intake.

Phosphorus matters in relation to calcium, not in isolation. The ideal Ca:P ratio for adult rabbits is between 1.5:1 and 2.0:1. A food with 1.1% calcium and 0.4% phosphorus has a ratio of approximately 2.75:1, which pushes toward calcium excess — even if neither number looks alarming on its own.

The practical takeaway: avoid any pellet with alfalfa in the first two ingredients if your rabbit is over 6 months old. This includes several large-brand "complete" formulas sold in grocery stores and large-format pet stores. Check the guaranteed analysis for calcium — anything above 1.0% for an adult maintenance formula is worth questioning. A formula designed around Timothy grass will typically show calcium between 0.35% and 0.65%, which is appropriate.

There's a second category of problem: muesli-style "mixed" foods — the brightly colored blends with dried fruit, seed mixes, and colored kibble pieces. Rabbits selectively eat the sweet, starchy components and leave the fibrous pieces, which means the nutrition label is irrelevant because the rabbit isn't eating the whole product. Studies from the Rabbit Welfare Association & Fund (RWAF) show that rabbits fed muesli-style diets have significantly higher rates of dental disease, cecal dysbiosis, and obesity compared to rabbits fed uniform pellets and hay. These products should be avoided entirely.

## Hay vs. Pellets: Getting the Ratio Right

Understanding what a "best rabbit food brand" actually means requires confronting an uncomfortable fact: pellets should not be the primary food source for adult rabbits. They are a supplement. The foundation of a healthy rabbit diet is unlimited grass hay — Timothy, orchard grass, meadow hay, or oat hay — which should constitute 80–85% of the rabbit's daily intake by volume and provide the bulk of its fiber.

The recommended pellet quantity for an adult rabbit is 1/4 cup per 5 lbs of body weight per day. A 6-lb adult rabbit gets approximately 1/3 cup of pellets daily — no more. Overfeeding pellets (which are calorie-dense at approximately 220–260 kcal per 100g depending on formula) leads to obesity, reduced hay consumption (because the rabbit is full), and subsequent fiber deficiency. The pellets should not be so calorie-dense or palatable that the rabbit preferentially eats them over hay.

This context matters when evaluating brands: a premium pellet isn't one that the rabbit finds delicious — it's one with a nutritional profile that complements unlimited hay without displacing it. Pellets with added molasses, dried fruits, or sugar content above 2% are engineered to be hyper-palatable, which means they compete with hay rather than supplementing it. The sugar content won't appear explicitly on most labels, but "sucrose," "cane molasses," or any form of dried fruit in the ingredient list signals a product designed for palatability over nutrition.

The right brand will have a bland, consistent pellet that a rabbit eats steadily but doesn't obsessively consume at the expense of hay. Some premium formulas include dried herbs (parsley, dill, thyme) in small quantities — this is fine because these herbs are non-caloric and non-palatable enough to not cause preferential eating. What you don't want is anything that makes the pellet more desirable than fresh timothy hay.

For rabbits under 6 months, the ratio shifts: unlimited alfalfa hay is appropriate at this stage because young rabbits need the higher protein and calcium for growth. Correspondingly, pellets with alfalfa as the base ingredient are appropriate for kits and juveniles, then should be transitioned to Timothy-based adult formula at 6–7 months over a 2–3 week transition period. Gut microbiome disruption during food transitions takes approximately 2 weeks to fully stabilize, so abrupt switches should always be avoided.

A third pellet formulation worth naming directly serves the senior rabbit (over 5 years): a formula with fiber at the high end of the range (22–25%), protein deliberately kept at 12–13% to reduce kidney stress, and calcium under 0.6%. Senior rabbits often have reduced kidney function, which makes the low-calcium, low-protein profile especially important. Fewer brands specifically formulate for senior rabbits, but the ones that do explicitly state it on packaging and adjust their guaranteed analysis accordingly.

## What to Avoid

**Alfalfa-based pellets for adult rabbits.** As covered: high calcium, high protein, low indigestible fiber. Fine for rabbits under 6 months. Problematic as a long-term adult diet.

**Muesli-style mixed foods.** The selective feeding problem means the guaranteed analysis is fictional for most rabbits. The RWAF has called for these products to be taken off the market entirely. Multiple peer-reviewed studies confirm elevated disease rates in rabbits fed these diets.

**Pellets with corn or soy as primary ingredients.** Corn is low-fiber and high-starch. Starch ferments rapidly in the cecum, disrupts bacterial populations, and promotes the growth of Clostridium and E. coli at the expense of beneficial bacteria. A small amount of soy (soybean meal as a protein supplement) is less problematic, but it should not be in the first three ingredients.

**Added sugars and fruit.** Rabbit digestive systems are not designed to process significant fructose loads. Dried cranberries, raisins, and banana chips are commonly added to "treat" pellet blends and are the equivalent of feeding a rabbit candy. Sugar content should be under 2% — and ideally, the ingredient list should show no added sweeteners at all.

**"Vitamin and mineral fortified" as the headline selling point.** This language typically signals that the base ingredients are nutritionally poor enough that the manufacturer had to compensate with a synthetic supplement pack. The best formulas derive their nutritional value from the quality of their primary ingredients, with minimal need for synthetic fortification beyond basic vitamin E and B-complex stability.

**Pellets that have been stored more than 6 months.** Fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K) degrade with storage. Check the "best by" date and avoid buying in quantities that you won't use within 6 weeks of opening — oxidation of fats accelerates after the bag is opened, and rancid pellets can cause digestive upset.

## Expert Perspective

Dr. Frances Harcourt-Brown, BVSc, FRCVS, veterinary surgeon and author of *Textbook of Rabbit Medicine* (Butterworth-Heinemann, 2002), has consistently argued in peer-reviewed literature that dental disease — the most common reason rabbits see vets — is directly linked to diet. In her clinical observations published in the *Journal of Exotic Pet Medicine*, she notes that rabbits fed high-hay, low-pellet diets show significantly less incisor and molar malocclusion because proper hay chewing provides the lateral jaw movement that grinds molar surfaces correctly. Pellets require mostly up-and-down jaw motion, which doesn't provide this benefit. Her recommended pellet quantity for a 5-lb adult rabbit is 2 tablespoons per day — which is substantially less than most feeding guides on commercial packaging suggest. The commercial packaging guideline, she notes, is optimized for sales volume rather than rabbit longevity.

## FAQ

### How much pellet food does a rabbit actually need per day?

The standard recommendation from rabbit-specialist veterinarians is 1/4 cup of pellets per 5 lbs of body weight, but Dr. Harcourt-Brown and the House Rabbit Society both advocate for the lower end of that range — approximately 2 tablespoons per 5 lbs for most adults. A 5-lb rabbit eating 1/4 cup of pellets daily is consuming roughly 55–65 kcal from pellets alone, which leaves limited appetite for hay. Most rabbits should derive the majority of their daily caloric intake from grass hay rather than pellets. Overfeeding pellets is one of the most common dietary mistakes rabbit owners make, typically because the rabbit visibly enjoys the pellets more than hay and owners interpret that preference as a need.

### Can rabbits eat Timothy hay-based pellets exclusively with no fresh vegetables?

Yes, for rabbits with health conditions that require a restricted diet, Timothy-based pellets plus unlimited grass hay constitutes a nutritionally adequate diet. Fresh leafy greens — romaine lettuce, kale, cilantro, parsley, dandelion greens — add beneficial phytonutrients and hydration but are not strictly required if the rabbit drinks adequate water (approximately 50–150ml per kilogram of body weight per day). Vegetables should be introduced gradually, at about 1 cup per 4 lbs of body weight per day, after 6 months of age. High-oxalate greens like spinach and beet greens should be rotated rather than fed daily to avoid oxalate accumulation.

### Is organic rabbit food worth the premium?

The evidence for organic pellets specifically improving rabbit health outcomes is limited. The more meaningful criteria are fiber percentage (22–25% crude fiber), protein level (12–16% for adults), and ingredient quality (grass hay as first ingredient). An organic pellet with 14% crude fiber and alfalfa as the primary ingredient is nutritionally inferior to a conventional pellet with 25% crude fiber and Timothy grass meal as first ingredient. That said, organic certification does reduce pesticide residue exposure, which has plausible long-term benefits even if clinical studies haven't established a direct link. If your chosen brand offers an organic version with the same nutritional profile, the premium may be worth it — but never sacrifice the nutritional profile for organic certification.

### When should I switch from junior to adult rabbit food?

The transition should begin at 6 months and be complete by 7 months. The shift is from alfalfa-based (appropriate for growth, higher protein and calcium) to Timothy-based (appropriate for maintenance, lower protein and calcium). Transition gradually over 2–3 weeks by mixing increasing proportions of adult formula into the juvenile formula — starting with 25% adult/75% juvenile, moving to 50/50 at week two, then 75/25 at week three, then full adult formula at week four. Abrupt diet changes can cause cecal dysbiosis within 24–48 hours, presenting as loose cecotropes, reduced appetite, or lethargy.

### Why does my rabbit eat its cecotropes and should I be concerned?

Cecotrope consumption is normal and necessary — cecotropes are soft, grape-cluster-shaped fecal pellets produced in the cecum that contain B vitamins, proteins, and beneficial bacteria produced during fermentation. Rabbits consume them directly from the anus, typically at night or early morning. You should never see them regularly in the enclosure. If you're finding cecotropes uneaten in the cage, it often indicates the rabbit is being overfed pellets (too full to reach down for cecotropes), is obese (physically unable to reach), or has dental pain making the awkward position uncomfortable. Uneaten cecotropes are a diagnostic signal, not a hygiene problem.

### Do rabbits need vitamin supplements in addition to commercial pellets?

A rabbit eating a high-quality Timothy-based pellet plus unlimited grass hay plus fresh leafy greens does not need vitamin supplements. Over-supplementation carries real risk: vitamin A toxicity (which causes bone deformities and liver damage in excess) and vitamin D toxicity (hypercalcemia) are documented in rabbits given supplements on top of already-fortified commercial food. The exception is vitamin C — rabbits synthesize their own and do not require dietary supplementation, unlike guinea pigs. If your vet identifies a specific deficiency via blood work, supplement for that condition specifically; do not use a general multivitamin as insurance.

### How long does an opened bag of rabbit pellets stay fresh?

An opened bag should be used within 6 weeks. After that, fat-soluble vitamins degrade measurably, fats begin oxidizing (producing rancid off-flavors and potentially harmful lipid peroxides), and moisture absorption can promote mold growth even without visible signs. Store pellets in an airtight container — not the bag — in a cool, dry location away from direct light. Temperatures above 77°F (25°C) accelerate oxidation. Do not buy larger bags to save money if you can't use them within 6 weeks of opening; the false economy negates the nutritional value you're paying for.

### Are pellet-free diets safe for rabbits?

A pellet-free diet — unlimited grass hay, fresh leafy greens, and occasional herbs — is supported by the House Rabbit Society and many rabbit veterinarians for healthy adult rabbits. In the wild, rabbits eat exclusively fibrous plant material; pellets are a convenience product designed around human feeding schedules rather than rabbit physiology. A pellet-free diet works if the rabbit gets true unlimited access to high-quality Timothy hay (not just a morning portion) and a varied selection of leafy greens daily. Where it fails is in households where hay is restricted or greens are inconsistent — in those conditions, pellets provide nutritional insurance. The diet should be evaluated by your vet with an annual health check including weight and dental assessment.
