## How Pet Dander Actually Moves Through Your Air

Most people think of pet dander as fur or visible flakes — something you can sweep up. The particles that cause allergic reactions are different. Fel d 1 (cat) and Can f 1 (dog) are glycoproteins secreted through sebaceous glands and salivary glands. When a pet grooms itself, these proteins coat the fur, dry, and flake off in particles ranging from about 1 to 10 microns. Many of those particles are buoyant enough that normal household air movement — walking across a room, opening a door, running a ceiling fan — relaunches them into breathable air.

Here's what makes this genuinely difficult: Fel d 1 is specifically engineered by cat biology (from an evolutionary standpoint) to be sticky. It adheres to clothing, upholstery, and walls. Studies published in *Clinical & Experimental Allergy* have found detectable Fel d 1 concentrations in homes with no cats whatsoever — transferred there from cat-owning visitors or coworkers. This stickiness is why air purifiers need to run continuously, not just when the cat is visibly active. The particles that settled on your couch three hours ago get relaunched repeatedly throughout the day.

The particles also distribute differently than smoke or cooking odors. Heavy dander settles faster in areas with low air circulation — corners, under furniture, in carpet fibers — while lighter particles stay suspended for two to four hours at typical indoor airflow. This means the purifier's placement matters nearly as much as its filtration quality: a unit tucked in a corner behind a sectional is partially filtering already-filtered air near the wall rather than pulling contaminated air from breathing zones.

For a HEPA filter to intercept these particles effectively, it needs to physically draw room air through its media. The critical metric is how many times per hour the purifier processes the total volume of the room — a value called ACH, or Air Changes per Hour. For allergen reduction, the American Lung Association recommends a minimum of 4 ACH. For someone with moderate-to-severe pet allergies, 5 ACH is a more practical target.

A 300-square-foot room with 8-foot ceilings contains approximately 2,400 cubic feet of air. To achieve 5 ACH in that room, you need a purifier moving at least 200 cubic feet per minute (CFM) — continuously. Most budget units marketed as "good for 300 sq ft" are rated at their maximum fan speed, which is often too loud for continuous daytime use. At the quieter medium setting most people actually run them at, effective coverage often drops by 30–40%.

## CADR: The Only Spec That Predicts Real Performance

CADR stands for Clean Air Delivery Rate, and it's the single most useful number on any air purifier's spec sheet. Developed by the Association of Home Appliance Manufacturers (AHAM) and measured by independent third-party testing, CADR tells you how many cubic feet of air — free of a specific pollutant — the unit delivers per minute at its highest fan setting. The three pollutants tested are smoke (0.1–0.3 microns), dust (0.5–3 microns), and pollen (5–11 microns).

For pet dander specifically, the dust CADR is your reference number. Pet allergen particles fall mostly in the 1–10 micron range, which overlaps heavily with the dust test category. A unit with a dust CADR of 200 is certified to deliver 200 cubic feet of dust-free air per minute — meaning it would cycle the air in a 200-square-foot room (8-foot ceiling) about 7.5 times per hour on high. The AHAM "2/3 rule" — sizing a purifier with a CADR at least two-thirds of the room's square footage — is a conservative baseline. For pet allergies, it's more accurate to match CADR numerically to room square footage: a 250-square-foot bedroom needs a dust CADR of at least 250.

What CADR does not measure: how quietly the unit runs at lower fan speeds, filter longevity, energy consumption, or performance against ultrafine particles below 0.1 microns (relevant for VOCs and some combustion byproducts, but less critical for dander specifically). It also doesn't account for actual room geometry — a long narrow room with furniture blocking airflow will underperform a rating designed for an open rectangular space.

One important caveat: ionizers and UV-C lamps are often bundled into air purifiers and marketed as allergen-fighting additions, but neither mechanism contributes meaningfully to CADR. The EPA notes that ozone-generating ionizers can irritate airways — counterproductive for someone already dealing with respiratory sensitivity. If a unit lists an impressive total CADR but relies partly on ionization to hit that number, the true mechanical filtration contribution is lower than advertised. Look for HEPA-only CADR scores when those are disclosed separately, or choose units that allow you to disable ionization entirely.

Energy consumption is also a real consideration for a device meant to run 24/7. Most effective units in the 200–300 CADR range draw between 45 and 85 watts at medium speed — roughly $4–8 per month at average US electricity rates ($0.12/kWh). Cheap units that spike to 150+ watts on high and can't run quietly on medium will either underperform or get switched off — which defeats the purpose entirely.

## True HEPA vs. H13 — Why the Distinction Matters for Allergies

"HEPA" is not a single standard. The term is used loosely in marketing, which creates meaningful differences between units that look similar on the shelf.

True HEPA (also called HEPA Type H11) captures 99.97% of airborne particles 0.3 microns or larger when tested under AHAM protocols. The 0.3-micron threshold is the "most penetrating particle size" — the hardest size for filter fibers to intercept mechanically — which is why it's used as the benchmark. Particles both smaller and larger than 0.3 microns are actually captured more efficiently due to different physical mechanisms (diffusion for small particles, impaction and interception for larger ones). Pet dander particles at 1–10 microns are well within HEPA's effective range.

H13 HEPA — the classification used in European EN 1822 testing standards, now increasingly cited by American manufacturers — sets a higher bar: 99.95% efficiency at the most penetrating particle size across the *entire filter face*, rather than just a representative sample. For most pet dander applications, True HEPA is sufficient. H13 matters more for ultrafine particles, hospital-grade filtration, or households where a member is immunocompromised.

What to watch out for: "HEPA-type," "HEPA-like," "99% HEPA," and "HEPA-style" are unregulated marketing terms that require no third-party certification. A filter labeled "HEPA-type" might capture 85% of particles at 0.3 microns — adequate for large dander fragments but insufficient for the smaller protein-coated particles that trigger the strongest allergic responses. If the packaging doesn't say "True HEPA" or cite a specific EN/AHAM certification, assume it underperforms.

Activated carbon filters are a separate layer — they don't affect CADR or particle filtration, but they're genuinely useful in pet households for neutralizing the volatile organic compounds (VOCs) responsible for pet odor. The effectiveness depends on carbon weight, not volume. A unit with 3–5 lbs of granular activated carbon will meaningfully reduce VOCs; a unit with a thin carbon-coated mesh is primarily marketing. Carbon filters typically require replacement every 3–6 months at continuous use, independent of the HEPA filter's lifespan (usually 12–18 months in a pet household, versus the 24-month baseline in clean-air environments — dander and fur accelerate clogging significantly).

Pre-filters extend HEPA life by capturing large particles — fur, visible dust — before they reach the fine-fiber membrane. In a home with a heavy-shedding dog or multiple cats, a washable pre-filter can add several months to HEPA filter life, reducing the roughly $40–80 annual replacement cost.

## Features That Sound Good But Don't Meaningfully Help

**Smart sensors and air quality indicators.** Most consumer-grade air quality sensors in purifiers use laser particle counters that measure particle density in the immediate vicinity of the unit — not the room as a whole. When the unit is running at full speed, the air directly at the intake reads clean, and the sensor throttles down. This defeats the purpose in a pet household where dander is continuously being resuspended. If you buy a smart purifier, set a minimum fan speed manually rather than relying entirely on auto mode.

**UV-C lamps.** UV-C light can damage the DNA of bacteria and viruses at sufficient exposure — in theory. In practice, residential air purifiers move air through the UV-C chamber too quickly for meaningful germicidal exposure time. A study published in *Indoor Air* (2012) found that residential UV-C lamps in forced-air systems required approximately 25 seconds of contact time for 99% bacterial inactivation; actual dwell time in most units is under one second. For pet dander — which is a protein particle, not a living organism — UV-C has zero effect.

**Negative ion generators.** Ionizers work by charging airborne particles so they clump together and fall to surfaces rather than remaining airborne. The problem: they don't remove allergens from your home, they redistribute them to floors, walls, and furniture — where they can be re-aerosolized. Some ionizers also produce ozone as a byproduct; the EPA and American Lung Association both caution against ozone-generating devices in homes with respiratory-sensitive occupants.

**"Washable" HEPA filters.** Water and HEPA media don't mix well. The fine glass fibers that create the tortuous filtration path can be permanently damaged by washing, reducing effectiveness in ways that aren't visible to the naked eye. Washable pre-filters are fine. Washable HEPA filters are a cost-saving feature that almost always compromises performance over time.

## FAQ

### How often do I need to replace the filter in a home with pets?

Plan for HEPA filter replacement every 10–12 months in a single-pet household, and every 6–8 months with multiple pets or heavy-shedding breeds. The standard manufacturer guidance of 18–24 months assumes clean-air environments — pet hair and dander clog filter media significantly faster. A visible indicator is useful, but a better signal is airflow: if the unit seems noticeably louder at the same fan setting (working harder to pull air through a clogged filter), it's overdue. Pre-filters, if washable, should be rinsed every 4–6 weeks.

### Where should I place the air purifier in a room?

Place the unit in the room where your pet spends the most time, ideally in the corner or against a wall — but elevated 1–3 feet off the floor if possible, since airflow is better above floor level. Avoid placing it behind furniture or in enclosed alcoves. In bedrooms, running the purifier on the same side as your pillow ensures allergen-reduced air is what you're breathing during sleep — the highest-exposure period for sensitive individuals. Distance from the pet's sleeping area matters: closer means faster allergen capture before particles disperse.

### Can an air purifier replace allergy shots or medication?

No — and it shouldn't be framed as a substitute. Allergen immunotherapy (allergy shots) and antihistamines address the immune response; air purifiers reduce the allergen load that triggers it. Used together, the combination is meaningfully more effective than either alone. A 2018 meta-analysis in *Environmental Health Perspectives* found that high-efficiency air filtration combined with allergen avoidance reduced asthma-related emergency department visits in sensitized children by approximately 30% compared to medication alone. Air purifiers are a reduction strategy, not a cure.

### Are there breeds of cats or dogs that are truly hypoallergenic?

Not technically, no. All cats produce Fel d 1; all dogs produce Can f 1 and at least four other documented allergenic proteins. "Hypoallergenic" breeds — Siberian cats, Balinese, Poodles, Portuguese Water Dogs — tend to shed less fur and may produce slightly lower total allergen loads, but peer-reviewed studies have not found a statistically significant difference in airborne allergen concentrations in homes with supposedly hypoallergenic versus standard breeds. The practical variability between individual animals of the same breed often exceeds the variability between breeds. An air purifier matters regardless of breed.

### What CADR do I need for an open-plan living area?

For open floor plans, calculate total square footage and use the 1:1 CADR-to-square-footage rule rather than the conservative 2/3 rule. A 600-square-foot open living and kitchen area needs a dust CADR of at least 600 — which typically means a tower-style or whole-room unit, or two mid-range units positioned at opposite ends of the space. Single units with CADR below 300 will struggle to achieve even 2 ACH in large open areas, which is insufficient for meaningful allergen reduction. Two well-placed units with a combined CADR of 600+ outperform one large unit in the center.

### Is it worth running the purifier at night when my pet is not in the bedroom?

Yes — and this is one of the most common setup mistakes. Dander that settled on bedding, pillowcases, and carpet during the day becomes airborne again through normal movement and temperature changes as the room cools at night. Cat allergen in particular has been measured at significant airborne concentrations in rooms the cat hasn't entered in 24–48 hours, due to ongoing resuspension from surfaces. Running the purifier continuously — including while you sleep — is more effective than intermittent use, and most units' sleep modes (fan speeds of 30–40 dB) are quiet enough to not disrupt sleep.

### Do air purifiers help with pet odor or just allergens?

HEPA filters alone don't address odor — they're mechanical filters that intercept particles, but pet odor comes from volatile organic compounds (gaseous molecules), which pass straight through fiber media. Activated carbon is what handles odor, and only units with a substantial carbon layer — minimum 2–3 lbs of granular carbon, not a thin carbon-coated mesh — make a meaningful difference. The two filtration types work on completely different principles: HEPA for particles and allergens, carbon for gases and odor. For a complete solution in a pet household, you need both.
