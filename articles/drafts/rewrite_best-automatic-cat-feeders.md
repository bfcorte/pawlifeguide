## What Actually Matters

### Portion accuracy — the entire reason for the device

A feeder that dispenses "approximately" the programmed amount is not doing its job. Quality feeders use a rotating drum or auger mechanism calibrated to deliver within 5% of the programmed portion. When evaluating feeders, look for verified portion accuracy data in reviews — owners who have weighed the actual dispensed amount against the programmed amount. This data exists in the review sections of well-reviewed feeders.

### Dual power: battery backup plus AC

A feeder that runs only on AC power fails when the power goes out. A feeder that runs only on batteries requires frequent replacement and will fail at an unpredictable moment. Dual power — AC primary with battery backup — is the reliability standard for a device that your cat's feeding depends on.

### Easy cleaning

Cat food, especially wet or semi-moist varieties, leaves residue that creates bacterial growth if not cleaned regularly. The tray, dispensing mechanism, and hopper should be removable and dishwasher-safe or easy to wash by hand. Feeders that require full disassembly to clean get cleaned less often — which is a hygiene problem.

### Anti-jam design for the specific kibble size you use

Most automatic feeders specify a compatible kibble size range (typically 0.3–0.5 inch diameter for standard designs). Irregular, flat, or very small kibbles jam more frequently than round, uniformly sized pieces. Check your kibble's shape and dimensions against the feeder's specifications. If you use a brand with irregular kibble (many freeze-dried varieties), verify compatibility before purchasing.

## What to Avoid

- Gravity feeders — they dispense continuously based on consumption, exactly replicating free-choice feeding
- Feeders without battery backup — single point of failure for your cat's scheduled meals
- Low-capacity feeders for multi-cat households — a 2-lb hopper feeding two cats requires daily refilling
- Feeders where the dispensing mechanism is not cleanable without disassembly
- Any feeder with sustained 1-star reviews specifically about jamming or malfunction

## FAQ

### How many meals should I program my cat to eat?

Two meals per day is the standard recommendation for adult cats — approximately 12 hours apart. For cats being transitioned from free-choice feeding, three smaller meals may ease the adjustment. Cats with hypoglycemia risk (rare but documented in some small breeds) may need 3–4 smaller meals. For weight management, two measured meals outperforms three smaller ones for most cats because the fasting period increases metabolic efficiency.

### Can I use an automatic feeder for wet food?

Some feeders are designed for wet food, but most are not. Standard automatic feeders use rotating drums or augers designed for dry kibble — wet food clogs these mechanisms and goes stale in the hopper. Specialized wet food feeders use ice packs or refrigerated compartments to keep food fresh between meals; these are a different product category. For cats that eat only wet food, the standard automatic feeder is not appropriate.

### What happens if the feeder jams?

The cat misses a meal — which is uncomfortable but not dangerous for a healthy cat over a single incident. Most quality feeders have jam detection sensors that alert the app or trigger an alarm. For cats with medical conditions requiring strict meal timing (diabetic cats on insulin), a feeder with jam detection and mobile alerts is essential, not optional.

### How do I transition my cat from free-choice to scheduled feeding?

Gradually. For the first week, fill the feeder with the cat's daily amount and set it to dispense 4–6 times throughout the day — effectively replicating free-choice availability with more structure. Over the following 2 weeks, reduce meal count to 3, then 2, while keeping the total daily amount constant. This prevents the panicked overbegging that occurs when free-choice cats suddenly face scheduled feeding.

### Can automatic feeders work for multiple cats?

Only if the cats eat separately. A feeder dispensing a portion for one cat doesn't know that another cat is eating it. For multi-cat households with weight differences or cats that need different diets, separate feeders in separate rooms at meal times is the only reliable approach. Microchip-activated feeders (like SureFeed) solve this at higher cost.
