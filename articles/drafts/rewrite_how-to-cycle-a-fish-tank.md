# How to Cycle a Fish Tank the Right Way — And Why Most People Skip the Step That Kills Fish

The majority of new fish deaths happen in the first three weeks — not from disease, not from bad fish, but from ammonia poisoning in an uncycled tank. Fish live in their own waste. Ammonia, produced continuously by fish respiration and decomposing organic matter, is directly toxic to gill tissue at concentrations as low as 0.02 mg/L. The nitrogen cycle — the biological process that converts ammonia into less harmful compounds — takes 4–8 weeks to establish from scratch. Most new fish owners skip it entirely because the tank looks fine and nobody told them the biology.

The process is not complicated, but it requires patience and measurement. Every shortcut that involves adding fish too early results in the same outcome: dead fish and a demoralized owner.

## Why This Matters

The nitrogen cycle establishes two bacterial colonies in your filter media. The first (Nitrosomonas species) converts toxic ammonia into nitrite. The second (Nitrospira species) converts nitrite into nitrate, which is far less toxic and removed by water changes. Without these colonies, ammonia builds unchecked to lethal levels within days of adding fish. With them, the tank becomes a stable, self-regulating biological system.

These bacteria grow on filter media — sponge, ceramic rings, bio balls — not in the water column. This is why the single most destructive mistake in aquarium keeping is cleaning the filter with tap water: the chlorine in tap water kills the bacterial colony and restarts the cycle from zero.

## Before You Start

- **Buy a liquid test kit** (not test strips): The API Master Test Kit measures ammonia, nitrite, nitrate, and pH accurately. Test strips have documented inconsistency at the low concentration ranges critical during cycling — 0.25 ppm nitrite looks like 0 on a strip but is lethal. Liquid kits are accurate at these ranges.
- **Get dechlorinator**: Sodium thiosulfate neutralizes chlorine and chloramine in tap water. Use one that also handles heavy metals (Seachem Prime is the standard). Every water change requires it.
- **Set up the tank completely**: Fill with dechlorinated water, run the filter and heater at the target temperature for 24 hours before starting the cycle. Beneficial bacteria establish fastest at 76–80°F.
- **Set realistic expectations**: The cycle takes 4–8 weeks from scratch. This is biology, not a design flaw. Plan accordingly.

## Step-by-Step Guide

### Step 1: Add an ammonia source — the bacteria need food to colonize

The bacteria that cycle your tank feed on ammonia. Without an ammonia source, they don't establish. Three options:

**Pure ammonia** (fastest, most controllable): Add enough to raise the tank to 2–4 ppm on the liquid test kit. Shake the bottle before testing — if it foams, it contains surfactants and won't work. Target: pure ammonia, no additives.

**Fish food method**: Add a pinch of fish food every 2 days. It decomposes, producing ammonia. Slower to ramp up but requires no special product.

**Seeding** (fastest overall — 1–2 weeks): Get a handful of gravel, a used filter sponge, or a used filter cartridge from an established tank. The bacterial colonies already living in that media colonize your new filter immediately. This cuts cycling time to 1–2 weeks and is the most reliable fast-cycle method.

### Step 2: Test every 2–3 days and record the results

The progression follows a predictable pattern when working correctly:

- **Week 1–2**: Ammonia rises to 2–4 ppm. This is correct — the bacteria haven't established yet.
- **Week 2–3**: Nitrite appears (0.25–1 ppm). Ammonia begins to drop. The first bacterial colony is establishing.
- **Week 3–5**: Nitrate appears. Nitrite drops. The second colony establishes.
- **Completion**: Ammonia = 0, Nitrite = 0, Nitrate detectable (5–20 ppm is typical).

If the progression stalls, check that: the temperature is above 70°F (bacteria establish slowly below this), the ammonia source is present, and the filter is running continuously.

### Step 3: Maintain the ammonia source throughout the cycle

When ammonia drops below 0.5 ppm but nitrite or nitrate hasn't appeared yet, add more ammonia source. The bacterial colony can crash if it runs out of food before fully establishing. Keep ammonia at 2–4 ppm until nitrite appears, then let it run naturally.

### Step 4: Confirm completion with back-to-back zero readings

On the day ammonia and nitrite both read zero with nitrate present: add 2 ppm ammonia. Test 24 hours later. If ammonia and nitrite are both back to zero within 24 hours, the cycle is complete — your tank can process its full ammonia load within a day. This verification step prevents premature fish additions after a single zero reading.

### Step 5: Do a 50% water change before adding fish, then add slowly

A large water change before fish addition reduces accumulated nitrate and resets the water chemistry. Add no more than 25–30% of your planned fish population at once — the bacterial colony is sized to the ammonia load it has been processing. Adding too many fish simultaneously creates more ammonia than the bacteria can handle, causing a partial cycle crash.

## Mistakes That Set You Back

- **Adding fish before ammonia and nitrite both read zero** — the most common cause of new fish death
- **Cleaning the filter with tap water** — destroys the bacterial colony; use tank water only
- **Using water too cold** — beneficial bacteria establish very slowly below 68°F
- **Using test strips instead of liquid kits** — inaccurate at the critical low ranges
- **Running the tank without the filter during cycling** — the bacteria live in the filter media, not the water

## FAQ

### Can I speed up the nitrogen cycle?

Yes — seeding with established filter media (a used sponge from an established tank, a handful of established gravel) is the most effective method and can cut cycling to 1–2 weeks. Commercial bacterial supplements (Tetra SafeStart Plus, Dr. Tim's One & Only) that contain live Nitrospira are the alternative if you don't have access to an established tank. Elevating temperature to 78–80°F also accelerates bacterial growth.

### My ammonia is rising but I see no nitrite yet — is something wrong?

No. The first bacterial colony (Nitrosomonas) typically takes 1–2 weeks to establish before nitrite appears. Keep adding ammonia source and testing every 2–3 days. Nitrite will appear when the first colony reaches sufficient population.

### What's the difference between nitrite and nitrate toxicity?

Nitrite causes methemoglobin formation ("brown blood disease") — it blocks oxygen transport in red blood cells. Affected fish gasp at the surface even in oxygenated water. Ammonia damages gill tissue directly — fish show redness at the gills, labored breathing, and lethargy. Nitrate is far less toxic; levels under 40 ppm are generally safe and managed with regular water changes.

### Do live plants affect cycling?

Plants absorb ammonia and nitrate, which can slow the visible nitrogen cycle progression (less ammonia available for the bacteria) but also help maintain lower parameters during the cycle. A heavily planted tank can achieve biological stability faster than a bare tank. Plants help — they don't replace the bacterial cycle.

### How do I know my test kit is giving accurate results?

Check the expiration date on all reagents — expired kits give false readings. Test a known concentration (some fish stores test water for free and can verify your results). The API Master Test Kit is the most widely validated in the hobby; results should be consistent between batches when reagents are fresh.
